Graffiti reading “FIGHT FASCISM” appears on the base of the Illinois Centennial Monument during the reconstruction of the Logan Square traffic circle.

View Print Details
Field Note

The monument in Logan Square has stood in the middle of the neighborhood for more than a hundred years. It was built to feel permanent — to sit above whatever happens around it. But in 2024, while the city was tearing up the traffic circle and rebuilding the streets, somebody put two words on the base in black spray paint: FIGHT FASCISM.

That’s what the photograph holds. Not a rally. Not a crowd. Just the monument, the message, and one person sharing the frame on an ordinary afternoon in the neighborhood. The official history and the lived one, sitting in the same shot.

Logan Square, Chicago — November 2024
The Record

The construction project around the monument lasted months. Roads were torn up, fences went around the park, pedestrian paths shifted week to week. The monument sat in the middle of all of it — supposedly preserved, but surrounded by heavy equipment, temporary barriers, and a neighborhood rerouting itself around the work.

During that period the graffiti appeared. Not a banner, not a projected slogan, not a sticker. Spray paint. Directly on the stone. FIGHT FASCISM. Two words on a hundred-year-old monument in the middle of a neighborhood that’s been changing for a decade.

That’s the image. That’s the record.

The Echo

FIRST DAY is the shout. RESIDUAL is what’s still sitting in the neighborhood years later. One photograph holds a quarter million people. The other holds a single line of spray paint and one person in the frame.

Different days. Different corners of the city. Same undercurrent. That’s why both of these are public record.
