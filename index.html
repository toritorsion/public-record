Why This Record Exists

Some photographs become public record the moment they're made.

FIRST DAY holds a quarter million people filling downtown Chicago for the Women's March on January 21, 2017 — the day after the inauguration. I made the frame from between two CTA train cars on the Green Line, one pass over the crowd at 1/2500 of a second.

RESIDUAL holds a single line of spray paint on a hundred-year-old monument in Logan Square — FIGHT FASCISM — with one person and a bicycle sharing the frame on an ordinary afternoon in 2024.

Different days. Different corners of the city. Same undercurrent.

Both images are proof that we were physically there. That the moment happened. That history gets made in public space, not just in headlines.

PUBLIC RECORD is where these photographs live as fine art prints — archival baryta paper, signed editions, printed in Chicago.

The Echo

Residual — Logan Square, 2024

FIRST DAY and RESIDUAL feel like two volumes of the same story.

One image holds thousands of voices at once. The other holds a single line of text and one person in frame. Different days. Different corners of the city. Same undercurrent: people refusing to be quiet about what’s happening around them.

That’s why I treat both of these as public record, not nostalgia.

If one or both of these frames feel like your memory of this era, I’d love for you to live with them off-screen. Pick your print, pick your size, and let your wall hold some of this history with you.
